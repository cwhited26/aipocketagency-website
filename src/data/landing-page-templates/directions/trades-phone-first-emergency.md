## Hero treatment
The entire first viewport is built around ONE goal: get the visitor to call. The phone number is the largest interactive element on the page — bigger than the headline, sticky in the header, repeated in the hero block as a giant tap-to-call button. Beneath it, a SHORT trust line ("Family-owned since 1998 · Licensed & insured · 4.9★ from 2,400+ Google reviews") and a "24/7 emergency" or "30-minute response" promise. The actual headline is secondary — it's there to confirm the visitor is on the right page (e.g. "Water damage cleanup in Knoxville · Available now") but the phone number is the visual centerpiece.

Background is usually a single hero photo of the actual crew or the business owner (not stock). Real faces drive trust in this category.

## Page layout
A short page — the visitor doesn't scroll much before either calling or leaving. Sections in order:

1. Hero with phone-number-as-centerpiece + trust line + sub-CTA quote form
2. Three-card "what we do" service grid (most common services for that trade)
3. Service-area map (literally a map of the metro area showing where they serve)
4. Recent jobs photo strip (real photos of actual work — before/afters, crew shots)
5. Real review carousel (Google Reviews / BBB / Angi pulled in directly)
6. Quick "request a quote" form (name, address, phone, brief description)
7. Footer with the phone number ONE MORE TIME

Mobile-first scroll length under 3 viewport heights. Above 3, conversion drops.

## Typography
- Display + body: a sturdy, legible sans (Inter, Plus Jakarta Sans, or Manrope) — heavy weights for the phone number (700-900), regular for body
- Phone number sizing: ENORMOUS on mobile — `text-5xl sm:text-6xl md:text-7xl` if it's the only thing in the hero block, or `text-4xl sm:text-5xl` if there's a small headline above
- Trust line uppercase, `tracking-wider`, smaller than body to feel like a stamp not a headline

## Color palette
- Background: white or very light gray (`#F9F9F7` / `#FAFAF9`) for trust and legibility on outdoor mobile screens
- Brand color: ONE strong accent — blue for plumbing/HVAC (trust), red/orange for emergency/restoration (urgency), green for landscape/lawn (nature)
- Phone number color: ALWAYS the brand accent — never gray or muted
- Trust badges: real brand colors of the third parties (Google blue, BBB orange, etc.) — DO NOT recolor brand badges, the recognition is the trust signal

## Visual motifs
- **Phone number as centerpiece** — the largest interactive element on the page, `tel:` link, tap-to-call works directly on mobile
- **Sticky header phone number** — phone visible at the top of every viewport on every scroll
- **Trust line with real numbers** — "Family-owned since 1998 · Licensed & insured · 4.9★ from 2,400+ Google reviews" — concrete numbers, not adjectives
- **24/7 / "30-minute response" promise** — bigger than the headline, smaller than the phone number
- **Real business-owner photo** — not stock. The visitor needs to see a real person
- **Service-area map** — actual map of the metro, not abstract icons
- **Real recent-jobs photo strip** — before/afters of actual jobs, not stock
- **Inline Google Reviews / BBB / Angi badges** — embedded review widgets, not decorative
- **Single brand accent color** used aggressively — phone number, CTAs, badges
- **NO video hero** — videos take time to load and the visitor needs the phone number in 1 second
- **NO cursor effects or scroll-driven theatrics** — the visitor is in a hurry, often on mobile, often outside

## When to use
- HVAC, plumbing, electrical, restoration (water/fire/mold), sewer/drain, garage door repair, locksmith
- Emergency-led services (24/7 availability is part of the pitch)
- Trades where Google Local / Google Ads / Nextdoor are the primary lead source
- Anyone whose customer is in active need RIGHT NOW (broken AC, flooded basement, no hot water)
- Anyone whose customer is over 45 and prefers to call rather than fill out forms

## When to NOT use
- Aspirational / lifestyle brands (this design reads as "blue collar" by intent)
- Anyone where the call is NOT the conversion (med spa, real estate where browsing matters more than calling)
- Anyone whose audience is digital-native and dislikes phone calls (younger buyers in some segments)
- Anywhere where slick design IS the trust signal (luxury services where Velar / Lithos directions fit better)
- B2B SaaS — wrong audience entirely

## Build complexity
LOW complexity. This direction ships fast. Tier 1 build can use this direction off the shelf in 3-4 focused hours once content and photos are in hand.
